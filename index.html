The essentials — everything you need to follow the game.

The Basics

Two teams of 11 players. 90 minutes split into two 45-minute halves. The team that scores the most goals wins. If tied after 90 minutes in the knockout stage, the match goes to extra time, then penalties.

Offside

A player is offside if they're closer to the opponent's goal line than both the ball and the second-to-last defender when the ball is played to them. You can't be offside from a throw-in, corner, or goal kick.

Yellow & Red Cards

A yellow card is a warning. Two yellows in the same match = automatic red card and ejection. A straight red card is given for serious fouls or dangerous play. A red-carded player cannot be replaced — their team plays with 10.

Fouls & Free Kicks

A foul gives the opposing team a free kick from the spot of the foul. A foul inside the penalty box results in a penalty kick — a one-on-one shot from 12 yards against the goalkeeper, no wall.

Extra Time & Penalties

Knockout matches that are level after 90 minutes go to 30 minutes of extra time (two 15-minute halves). If still tied, a penalty shootout decides — each team takes 5 kicks, then sudden death if still level.

VAR

Video Assistant Referee. Officials can review goals, red cards, penalty decisions, and mistaken identity using video replay. The on-field referee makes the final call after consulting the VAR team.

Substitutions

Each team can make up to 5 substitutions per match (from a bench of 15). A player who comes off cannot return. Referees add extra time at the end of each half to account for stoppages.

The Goalkeeper

The only player allowed to handle the ball (within their penalty area). They wear a different kit color to distinguish themselves. A goalkeeper can be outfield if needed — it happens, occasionally.

The 2026 World Cup is the biggest ever — 48 teams, 104 matches, three host countries. Here's how it all fits together.

48 Teams, 12 Groups

The field is split into 12 groups of 4 teams (A through L). Every team plays the other three in their group once — 3 points for a win, 1 for a draw, 0 for a loss.

Who Advances

The top two teams from each group advance automatically (24 teams). The 8 best third-place finishers across all 12 groups also advance, giving 32 teams in the knockout round.

Tiebreakers

If teams are level on points, the tiebreaker order is: goal difference → goals scored → head-to-head result → head-to-head goal difference → FIFA ranking. Rarely gets past goal difference.

Round of 32

The first knockout round. 32 teams, single elimination. Win or go home — no second chances. Group winners are seeded to face a runner-up or third-place team from a different group.

Round of 16 → Quarterfinals

16 survivors play for a spot in the quarterfinals, then 8 teams fight for the final four. Every match from here is sudden death — tied after 90 minutes means extra time and potentially penalties.

Semifinals & Third Place

The four remaining teams play two semifinals. Losers meet in a third-place playoff (July 18, Miami). Winners advance to the final. Third place still matters — it's a World Cup bronze medal.

The Final

July 19, 2026 at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey — capacity 82,500. The biggest single sporting event on the planet. 90 minutes, extra time if needed, then penalties if still level.

Three Host Countries

For the first time, three nations co-host: the United States (11 venues), Mexico (3 venues), and Canada (2 venues). Group stage matches are spread across all 16 cities; later rounds concentrate in the US.

Where to Watch

In the US: FOX/FS1 in English (YouTube TV, Fubo, Hulu + Live TV) and Telemundo/Peacock in Spanish. Search below for broadcasters in any of the 48 participating countries.

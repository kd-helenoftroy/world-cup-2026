The essentials: everything you need to follow the game.

The Basics

Two teams of 11 players. 90 minutes split into two 45-minute halves. Most goals wins. If tied in a knockout match, it goes to extra time and potentially a penalty shootout. Group stage draws just split the points.

Offside

A player is offside if they're ahead of the last defender when the ball is played to them. If the ball ricochets off a defender to an offside-positioned attacker, play stops. But if an opponent intercepts a pass intended for an offside player, play continues. You can't be offside from a throw-in, corner, or goal kick.

Penalty Kick, Corner Kick, Free Kick: What's the Difference?

Penalty kick: Awarded for a foul inside the 18-yard box. One player vs. the keeper from 12 yards; all other players stay outside until the kick is taken.

Free kick: Awarded anywhere on the pitch for fouls or offsides. Defenders form a wall 10 yards away.

Corner kick: Awarded when a defender sends the ball out past their own goal line. Taken from the corner flag, roughly 20 yards from goal.

Throw-in: When the ball exits along the sideline, the opposing team restarts with a two-handed throw from behind the head.

Yellow & Red Cards

A yellow card is a warning. Two yellows in the same match = automatic red and ejection. A straight red is for serious fouls or dangerous play; the team plays with 10 and cannot bring on a replacement. In this tournament, two yellow cards across group stage matches triggers a one-match suspension. Card counts reset after the group stage and again after the quarterfinals.

Handballs

A handball is called when a player's hand (fingertips to armpit) deliberately or unnaturally obstructs the ball or leads to a goal. FIFA's interpretation differs slightly from domestic leagues, which causes some confusion. A handball in the box = penalty. Outside the box = free kick.

Extra Time & Penalty Shootout

Knockout matches tied after 90 minutes go to two 15-minute halves of extra time. Teams get one additional substitution (6 total). If still level, a penalty shootout: each team picks 5 takers, alternating shots. If tied after all 5, it goes to sudden death until someone wins.

VAR

Video Assistant Referee reviews goals, red cards, penalties, and mistaken identity for "clear and obvious errors." At this World Cup, VAR has expanded to also review corner vs. goal kick calls and second yellow card incidents. All VAR decisions are managed from a central hub in Dallas.

Substitutions

Each team can make up to 5 substitutions per match (6 in extra time). Players who come off cannot return. The fourth official signals added time at the end of each half to account for stoppages, substitutions, and VAR delays; it can run long.

Water Breaks

New at this World Cup: mandatory 3-minute cooling breaks happen around the 30th and 75th minutes of each half, depending on the flow of play. Designed to combat heat in stadiums across the US and Mexico.

Time-Wasting Rules

Referees can enforce timers: 5 seconds for throw-ins and dead-ball restarts, 10 seconds for substitutions. Players who take too long on a substitution cannot enter for one minute. Injured players who need the game stopped must stay off for at least one minute before returning.

The Referees

Three on-pitch officials: the center referee calls fouls and issues cards; two assistants track offside and call corner vs. goal kicks. A fourth official manages substitutions and bench conduct. The 2026 World Cup uses 52 referees with whistles and 20 dedicated VAR officials.

The Goalkeeper

The only player allowed to handle the ball; only inside their own penalty area. They wear a different kit to stand out. During a penalty kick, the keeper can move freely along the goal line while the shooter runs up.

Tiebreakers

If teams finish level on points, FIFA works through 7 sequential factors: (1) goal difference, (2) goals scored, (3) head-to-head record, (4) head-to-head goal difference, (5) fair play record (fewest cards), (6) drawing of lots, (7) FIFA ranking. It rarely goes past goal difference; but when it does, it's drama.

Third-Place Playoff

The two semifinal losers play for bronze on July 18 in Miami.

Three Host Countries

For the first time, three nations co-host: the United States (11 venues), Mexico (3 venues), and Canada (2 venues). Group stage matches are spread across all 16 cities; later rounds concentrate in the US.
